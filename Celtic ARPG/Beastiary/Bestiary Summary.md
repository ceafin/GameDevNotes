# Celtic ARPG Bestiary — Merged Summary & Corrections

This note consolidates the three bestiary working files into one reference, and flags places where a creature's folklore basis was misstated (not places where you took deliberate creative license for gameplay — those are left alone).

## 1. What each source file is for

- **Base Beast Raw List.md** — unfiltered brainstorm: one-line flavor pitch per creature, often with a loose Zelda-enemy comparison baked into the sentence.
- **Base Beast List Classed-Tagged.md** — the same ~84-creature roster sorted by encounter role, using tags **OW** (overworld), **DG** (dungeon), **MB** (miniboss), **B** (boss), **SB** (story/arc boss).
- **Gaelic Analogs for Classic Zelda Enemy Types.md** — a subset of the roster mapped onto specific Zelda archetypes (Octorok, Moblin, Darknut, Wizzrobe, Lynel, Hinox, Stalfos, ReDead, Wallmaster, Armos, Like Like, Keese/Peahat), plus a fully worked "first region" starter roster (8 overworld, 8 dungeon, 3 minibosses, 2 bosses) across two islands.

- **Bosses:** Manx Barrow Wight (Dungeon 1), Muirdris (Dungeon 2).

## 5. Folklore corrections

These are cases where the *creature's real-world folkloric basis* was misstated across the source files (as opposed to a deliberate gameplay reinterpretation, which is fine and left untouched).

- **Leprechaun** — In Irish folklore the leprechaun is a solitary cobbler who *guards his own* hidden gold and is notoriously hard to catch; if you *do* catch one, he must grant a reward (often three wishes) to be released, and will trick his way out if you look away. He is not traditionally a mugger who steals items from passersby. If you want the "steals your rupees and flees" mechanic, that's a fine game beat, but frame it as your own twist rather than folklore — or better, keep the reward-for-catching angle (catch him to get an item/currency bonus) since that's the actual legend and could be a more interesting mechanic than a generic thief.
- **Selkie** — Selkies are almost always the *victims* in their myths: a human steals a seal-person's skin (usually while they're dancing on the shore in human form) and forces them into marriage; the selkie pines for the sea and escapes the moment they recover their skin. They are gentle, melancholic figures, not deceptive tricksters. Casting them as "Goriya-style" bait-and-switch enemies inverts the actual story — consider reworking them as a tragic NPC/rescue quest rather than an ambush enemy.
- **Fear Gorta** — The "man of hunger" is a beggar-spirit tied to famine memory (especially the Great Famine); tradition holds that those who show him charity are rewarded with good luck, not that he inherently harms or drains people. He's a generosity test, not a hazard. If ignoring him should cost the player something, that's your addition — worth noting in design docs as a deliberate departure rather than "he drains you" being the folklore default.
- **Lámh Dhearg ("Red Hand")** — This isn't a monster or ghost in the source legend. The Red Hand of Ulster (Ó Néill heraldry) comes from a story where a chieftain racing by boat to claim land cuts off his own hand and throws it ashore first to win the claim — it's a legend about ambition and sacrifice, not a grabbing spectral hand. Using the name for a Wallmaster-style grab trap works visually (a disembodied hand), but it's a full reinvention of the source story, not an adaptation of it — worth either renaming this enemy or being explicit internally that only the imagery ("a red hand") was borrowed, not the legend.
- **Bean Sídhe / Bean Nighe** — These are two distinct figures folded into one entry. The **Bean Sídhe** (banshee) wails to foretell a death but is not typically seen performing an action. The **Bean Nighe** ("washer at the ford") is a separate figure seen washing the blood-stained burial clothes of those about to die — she can be reasoned with or even grants boons/curses if approached correctly (approaching from behind and getting between her and the water gives you power over her). They're related death-omen spirits but have different behaviors; worth splitting into two entries if you want the Bean Nighe's unique "sneak up on her" interaction hook.
- **Manx Barrow Wight** — "Wight" isn't a Gaelic or Manx word; it's Old English (*wiht*, "creature/being"), popularized as an undead-guardian term by Tolkien's barrow-wights. Every other entry in the roster uses a native Irish/Scots Gaelic/Manx name — this one is the odd one out linguistically. Not wrong to use (it reads clearly to players), just worth knowing it's an English/Tolkien loanword sitting inside an otherwise Gaelic-named roster, in case naming consistency matters to you.
- **Crom's Hounds** — There's no attested legend of Crom Cruach having hunting dogs; Crom Cruach is a pagan idol god (a gold-and-stone figure surrounded by lesser stone idols) tied to a human-sacrifice legend and his banishment by St. Patrick — no serpent or hound mythology attaches to him in the sources. "Crom's Hounds" as branded warband dogs is a plausible invented extension of "creatures serving a dark cult," but it isn't drawn from folklore the way most of the roster is — worth a note internally so it doesn't get mistaken for a sourced legend later.
- **Nuckelavee** *(not an error — noted for completeness)* — Already correctly caveated in your files as "Gaelicized." Nuckelavee is genuinely Orcadian folklore (Orkney was Norse-settled, not Gaelic), so importing it and flagging the adaptation, as you already did, is the right call.
